# Judge Preferences — Interview Edition

What ISEF/STS judges actually look for when they walk up to a poster, what they ask, and what loses points fast. This is the interview-focused subset; for the full poster-design version see the `science-fair-judge` skill's reference of the same name.

---

## 1. The first 30 seconds

Judges form an impression in the first 30 seconds. The student should be ready for:

- A handshake and eye contact (don't be staring at the poster).
- "Tell me about your project" — this triggers the 2-minute elevator pitch. Not 5 minutes. Not 30 seconds.
- A scan of the poster while the student talks. Judges read the title, hypothesis, and headline result first; they listen second.

**What loses points immediately:**
- Reading from notes or the poster.
- A monotone recital that's clearly memorized.
- Failing to introduce the *problem* before the *method* (judges don't yet know why they should care).
- Hands in pockets, blocking the poster, looking down.

**What gains points immediately:**
- A one-sentence hook ("Stars in deep magnetic minimum may explain why the Sun seems quieter than its peers — I found 795 candidates in Kepler data") before the method.
- Confident pointing at the headline figure, not the title.
- Smile, energy, and interest in *engaging* with the judge — not surviving them.

## 2. The interview structure judges follow

Most ISEF interviews follow this implicit pattern in 12–15 minutes:

| Stage | Time | What the judge is doing |
|---|---|---|
| **Pitch** | 0–2 min | Listening to elevator pitch; forming hypothesis about depth |
| **Probing** | 2–6 min | Asking layered questions to test methodology |
| **Stress test** | 6–10 min | Asking critical / hypothetical questions to probe limits |
| **Big picture** | 10–12 min | Impact, future work, broader significance |
| **Wrap** | 12–14 min | "Any questions for me?" / handshake |

The student should not try to control the interview — judges resent that — but should be aware of which stage they're in. If the judge is still in **probing** at minute 10, the project is going well and they want to dig deeper. If they've moved to **wrap** at minute 5, that's a bad sign.

## 3. What separates Grand Award winners from qualifiers

Across categories, the qualitative differences in interview behavior are remarkably consistent:

| Behavior | Qualifier | Grand Award level |
|---|---|---|
| Self-criticism | "My results confirmed the hypothesis." | "Results are consistent with the hypothesis but constrained by [3 specific limitations]." |
| Knowledge of alternatives | "I used method X." | "I used X; the alternatives were Y and Z. I chose X because [tradeoff]." |
| Numbers fluency | Fumbles for numbers in poster. | Recites all key numbers + uncertainties from memory. |
| Limitations | "Sample size was a limitation." | "Three foundational limitations, ranked: A is most serious because... B affects only [subset]... C is bounded by [a different test]." |
| Future work | "We need more data." | "Three concrete next steps with timelines: by 2027, by 2028, by 2030." |
| Mentor attribution | "My mentor helped a lot." | "My mentor advised on theoretical framing; I designed the pipeline, ran the analysis, validated the results." |
| Ownership of weakness | Defensive when challenged. | "That's a fair point — let me reconsider. I think the strongest counter is..." |

The single most distinguishing behavior: **a Grand Award candidate names their own weaknesses before the judge asks.**

## 4. The five questions virtually every judge asks

These are the canonical five. The student must be flawless on these:

1. **"Can you walk me through your project in 2 minutes?"** — the elevator pitch.
2. **"What would you do differently if you started over?"** — the maturity test. "Nothing" is fatal.
3. **"What are the limitations of your study?"** — must have 3+ specific, ranked limitations.
4. **"How does this compare to prior work?"** — must know at least 2–3 foundational papers.
5. **"What's next?"** — must have concrete next steps, not "more research."

If the student can't answer all five fluently, no other prep matters.

## 5. The probing techniques judges use

### 5a. Layered questioning
Judge starts simple, then drills into the real question:
- L1: "What is X?"  *(warmup, sanity check)*
- L2: "How does X work in your project?"  *(connecting concept to project)*
- L3: "Why did you choose X over Y?"  *(testing alternatives awareness)*
- L4: "What would happen if X failed?"  *(testing critical understanding)*

L4 is where students who don't really understand fall apart. Prep for L4 on every major method choice.

### 5b. Hypothetical perturbation
"What if your noise threshold were doubled?" / "What if your sample size were 100 instead of 3000?" — tests whether the student understands *why* their choice was right, not just *what* the choice was.

### 5c. The "explain it simply" test
"Imagine I'm a chemist visiting your physics project — explain your method in two sentences." Tests real understanding. If the student can't simplify, they don't fully grasp it.

### 5d. The conclusion-vs-limitations tension
This is the most surgical question type. Judge reads the conclusion ("X is true"), then reads the limitations ("but Y might invalidate X"), then asks: **"Reconcile these."** A weak student picks one and abandons the other. A strong student says: "The conclusion is bounded — it holds *given* my assumption that Y doesn't dominate, and here's the evidence Y is small."

### 5e. The "why hasn't anyone done this?" question
For novel methods: "If your approach works so well, why hasn't this been published before?" Tests whether the student understands the technical or historical reason. Strong answer: "It became feasible only when Kepler's 4-year baseline arrived." Weak answer: "Other people aren't as creative."

## 6. Common pitfalls that lose points fast

### 6a. The "nothing wrong" trap
"What would you change?" → "Nothing." This is the single biggest red flag. Judges immediately downgrade scientific maturity.

### 6b. The mentor-driven project signal
Phrases that flag a mentor-driven project:
- "My mentor told me to..."
- "I'm not sure why we did that — my mentor decided."
- "The lab uses this software."
- "It's standard in the lab."

If the project is genuinely mentor-heavy, the recovery is: **be specific about your contributions.** "My mentor framed the question; I designed the pipeline, ran the analysis, validated the results, and wrote up the conclusions."

### 6c. Overclaim at the conclusion
"My results prove..." — judges hate "prove." Replace with "demonstrate," "support," "are consistent with."

### 6d. P-value worship
"My p-value is 10⁻⁸² so the result is real." Wrong framing. Judges want: effect size + multiple convergent tests + addressed assumption violations.

### 6e. Generic future work
"More data would help" / "We need more research" — judges immediately tune out. Replace with: "By 2027 we could test [specific thing]; by 2028 we could observe [specific signature]."

### 6f. Defensive posture
When challenged, students sometimes get argumentative. This is fatal. The right move is collaborative: "That's a fair point — here's how I think about it..."

## 7. Category-specific judge expectations

### Physics / Astronomy
- Error propagation is mandatory. Judges *will* ask "how does measurement error in X propagate to your final result?"
- Know your theory comparison. "How does this compare to [theoretical prediction]?"
- Units are scrutinized. Wrong units = immediate red flag.
- Selection effects are scrutinized. "What stars/objects are you missing?"

### Biology / Biomedical
- Sample size justification. "Did you do a power analysis?"
- Positive and negative controls. "What was your negative control?"
- Replicates. "Biological replicates vs technical replicates?"
- Statistical assumptions. Mann-Whitney vs t-test choice.

### Computer Science / ML
- Baseline comparison is mandatory. "What's the simplest baseline that does this?"
- Train/test split discipline. "How did you avoid leakage?"
- Overfitting checks. "How do you know you're not just memorizing?"
- Reproducibility. "Random seed? Hyperparameter choice?"

### Engineering
- Design tradeoffs. "Why this design over alternatives?"
- Failure modes. "What can go wrong, and what's the worst case?"
- Cost / feasibility. "What does this cost to scale?"
- Iteration. "Show me your design history — what did v1, v2, v3 look like?"

### Math
- Proof rigor. "Walk me through the key proof step."
- Generality. "Does this generalize? Where does it break?"
- Connection to existing theory. "How does this relate to [foundational result]?"

### Environmental
- Real-world calibration. "What does this mean for [policy / community]?"
- Stakeholder awareness. "Who is the audience for this work?"
- Scaling. "Can this be deployed at scale?"

## 8. STS-specific differences

Regeneron STS judges weight more heavily on:
- **Scientific maturity** (Part F in our framework). They WILL ask the conclusion-vs-limitations question, often as their first hard question.
- **Independent thought** ("What's the most original contribution *you* made?"). Mentor-driven projects fail STS faster than ISEF.
- **Broader impact**. STS values "what does this mean for the field?" more than ISEF.
- **Curiosity beyond the project**. STS finalists often get asked about *other* science topics they find interesting. ISEF judges rarely do this.

For STS prep, weight Parts F and G higher than the default.

---

## 9. Red flags judges silently note

These behaviors don't get called out in the moment but lower scores:
- Calling the judge "Doctor" excessively (sycophancy reads as insecurity).
- Saying "obviously" or "trivially" — students rarely use these correctly.
- Eye contact only with the senior judge if there are multiple judges.
- Dismissing the question ("That doesn't really apply here").
- "Like" "um" "you know" excessive verbal fillers — audible nervousness.
- Touching the face / hair repeatedly (nervous tic; visible).
- Looking at watch / phone (signals impatience with the judge).
