# ISEF Judging System — Official Rubric & Process Reference

> Pre-researched reference. Agents should use this as ground truth instead of web-searching for basic rubric info. Web search only for year-specific updates or rule changes after 2026.

## Scoring Rubric (100 Points Total)

ISEF uses **two distinct rubrics** — one for Science projects, one for Engineering.

### Science Project Rubric

| Category | Points | What Judges Evaluate |
|----------|--------|---------------------|
| **I. Research Question** | 10 | Clear, focused, testable purpose; identifies contribution to field |
| **II. Design & Methodology** | 15 | Well-designed plan; variables/controls defined, appropriate, complete |
| **III. Execution: Data Collection, Analysis & Interpretation** | 20 | Systematic data collection; reproducibility; proper math/stats; sufficient data |
| **IV. Creativity & Potential Impact** | 20 | Significant creativity; impact on science, society, technology, economy, or environment |
| **V. Presentation** | 35 | Poster (10 pts) + Interview (25 pts) |

### Engineering Project Rubric

| Category | Points | What Judges Evaluate |
|----------|--------|---------------------|
| **I. Research Problem** | 10 | Practical need; solution criteria; constraints |
| **II. Design & Methodology** | 15 | Alternatives explored; solution identified; prototype developed |
| **III. Execution: Construction & Testing** | 20 | Working prototype; multi-condition testing; engineering skill |
| **IV. Creativity & Potential Impact** | 20 | Same as Science |
| **V. Presentation** | 35 | Same as Science (Poster 10 + Interview 25) |

### Weight Distribution Summary

| Component | Points | Percentage |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| Project substance (Categories I-III) | 45 | 45% |
| Creativity & Impact (Category IV) | 20 | 20% |
| Presentation total (Category V) | 35 | 35% |
| — of which Poster | 10 | 10% |
| — of which Interview | 25 | 25% |

**Key insight:** The interview alone (25%) is worth more than any single project-substance category. Creativity (20%) equals Execution (20%). The poster is the least weighted component (10%).

### Interview Scoring (25 Points)

Judges evaluate:
1. Clear, concise, thoughtful responses to questions
2. Understanding of basic science relevant to the project
3. Understanding of interpretation and limitations of results
4. Degree of independence in conducting the project
5. Recognition of potential impact
6. Quality of ideas for further research
7. For teams: contributions by ALL members

## Judge Selection & Background

- **All Grand Award judges:** PhD or equivalent, and/or 6+ years relevant professional experience
- Typically research scientists, engineers, university faculty, industry professionals
- Background screening via Sterling Volunteers; ethics statements signed
- Assigned to one of 22 ISEF categories based on expertise
- Serve Grand Award OR Special Award judging, never both
- ~1,000+ Grand Award judges for ~1,800 finalists
- Each project judged at least 4 times by different judges

## The Judging Process

### Critical Insider Insight: Scores Are NOT the Final Answer

**The most important thing to understand:** Judges submit a SINGLE 0-100 integer score (not per-criterion scores). This score is then normalized across judges to create ordinal rankings. However, **scores alone do NOT determine awards** — the caucus discussion is where actual winners are decided.

**Why scores are not enough:**
- Different judges have different scoring distributions (some score 70-90, others 80-100)
- ~5% of judges never score below 80, ~5% never score above 80
- Two-stage normalization process eliminates judge bias
- But even after normalization, scores are just the starting point for caucus discussion

### Timeline (Based on ISEF 2026, Phoenix)

| Time | Activity |
|------|----------|
| **Tuesday** | Judge registration; optional board review without students (until 9 PM) |
| **Wednesday 8 AM** | Check-in, breakfast with category team, calibration |
| **Wednesday 9:30 AM-12:15 PM** | Judging Period 1 — 15-minute interview sessions (actually 10-12 min conversation) |
| **Wednesday 1:15-4:00 PM** | Judging Period 2 — More interviews |
| **Wednesday 4:15-5:15 PM** | Period 19 — "My5 Projects" additional interviews |
| **Wednesday 5:30-6:30 PM** | Period 20 — Final "My5 Projects" interviews |
| **Wednesday evening** | Category caucus to determine winners |

### "My5 Projects" System

Judges select their 5 favorite projects during the day for additional interviews in periods 19-20. This is CRUCIAL for award determination:
- Allows judges to revisit borderline projects
- Provides additional conversation time to resolve uncertainties
- Often determines which projects make the "Pool" for caucus discussion
- Judges can only select projects they've already interviewed
- Selections must be made by end of Period 18

### Interview Format
- **Duration:** 15-minute slots, but realistically only 10-12 minutes of actual conversation (transition time between judges)
- Student opens with brief 1-2 minute oral summary (no slides, no formal presentation)
- Judge asks questions, views notebook/work logs
- Discussion-based — judges form opinions via questions, not rehearsed speeches
- Each project visited at least 4 times by different judges
- **Critical:** Do NOT let the student monopolize the conversation with a rehearsed presentation. Judges need time to ask questions that probe understanding.

### Grand Award vs Special Award

| Aspect | Grand Award | Special Award |
|--------|-------------|---------------|
| Organized by | Society for Science | 50+ external orgs (NSA, ACM, IEEE, etc.) |
| Judges | ~1,000+ total | Min 3 per organization |
| Rubric | Official 100-pt ISEF rubric | Each org sets own criteria |
| Awards | 1st-4th per category | Varies (scholarships, cash, etc.) |
| Overlap | Can win both Grand + Special Awards | |

### Award Amounts (Per Category)

| Place | Cash Award |
|-------|-----------|
| 1st | $5,000 |
| 2nd | $2,000 |
| 3rd | $1,000 |
| 4th | $500 |

Top Awards (from 1st place winners): Yancopoulos Innovator ($100K), Regeneron Young Scientist ($75K x2), Gordon Moore ($50K), and others. Total: ~$9 million distributed annually. ~600 of ~1,800 finalists receive awards (~33%).

### What Separates Award Tiers

- **1st Place:** Exceptional across all 5 criteria; novel question; rigorous methodology; clear impact; deep independent understanding
- **2nd-3rd Place:** Strong across most criteria; minor gaps in one area; solid understanding
- **4th Place:** Good work with clear merit; notable weakness in one category
- **No Award (~60%):** Not competitive at international level; methodology issues, limited scope, or weak interview

## The Caucus Process — Where Awards Are Actually Decided

### Understanding Caucus

**Caucus is THE decision-making process.** Scores are just input; the actual award determination happens through live discussion among category judges.

### Two-Phase Caucus Process

**Phase 1: Growing the Pool**
- Goal: Build a pool of projects that deserve consideration for awards
- Starts with normalized scores as initial sorting
- Judges advocate for projects they believe are award-worthy
- Projects can be added to the pool through discussion even if scores aren't top
- Focus: "Should this project receive ANY award?"

**Phase 2: Selecting Awardees**
- Goal: Determine specific award levels (1st-4th place)
- More detailed discussion of each project in the pool
- Judges make cases for specific placements
- Majority vote determines final awards

### Three-Table System

During caucus, projects are physically organized into three groups:

| Table | Meaning | What Happens |
|-------|---------|--------------|
| **Awarded** | Final award placement decided | Moved here after majority vote |
| **Pool** | Under consideration | Being discussed for placement |
| **Rejected** | Not receiving an award | Removed from consideration |

**Critical nuance:** A project in "Pool" is still very much alive — advocates can persuade other judges to move it to "Awarded." The process is deliberative, not purely score-driven.

### Majority Vote Rule

- All award placements require majority vote
- Judges can and do change positions based on discussion
- Strong advocates can overcome lower scores with compelling arguments
- This is why interview quality matters so much — judges need material to advocate for you

### Role of Interview Performance

Your interview affects caucus in two ways:
1. **Initial score** — Influences whether you make the Pool
2. **Advocacy material** — Judges who interviewed you can argue your case if they understood your work deeply

**Key insight:** A judge who interviewed you and "got it" is your biggest asset in caucus. They can explain nuances other judges missed from just reading the abstract.

## Recent Changes (2024-2026)

### AI Usage (New)
- AI may be used but must be **properly cited and disclosed**
- Integrity section of International Rules modified to include AI
- Judges probe student's understanding of AI-assisted components
- Educator Guide published with AI rules context

### Project Duration
- Maximum **one year of work** (12 months continuous)
- Research >18 months before ISEF cannot be included

### Terminology
- "Designated Supervisor" → "Direct Supervisor" (same role, name change only)

### Emphasis Areas
- Reproducibility is core under Execution
- Statistical rigor explicitly mentioned in rubric
- AI/ML projects judged on student's genuine understanding
- Independence probed heavily in interviews
- Broader impact gaining emphasis under Creativity & Impact

## Score Normalization — How Judge Bias Is Eliminated

### The Problem: Judge Inconsistency

Different judges use different parts of the 0-100 scale:
- Some spread scores from 70-95
- Others cluster between 80-100
- ~5% never score below 80
- ~5% never score above 80

Raw scores cannot be compared directly across judges.

### Two-Stage Normalization Process

**Stage 1: Initial Normalization**
- Converts each judge's raw scores to a common scale
- Accounts for individual judge's mean and standard deviation
- Creates comparable scores across all judges

**Stage 2: Renormalization**
- After caucus discussion and score adjustments
- Re-normalizes all scores to maintain fairness
- Ensures final rankings reflect consensus, not individual judge bias

### What This Means for Students

- Your raw score matters less than your **relative position** among other projects
- A "harsh" judge doesn't hurt you if they're consistent with all their scores
- The key is standing out in YOUR judge's scoring distribution
- This is why interview quality is critical — it's your chance to differentiate yourself

### Practical Implication

Don't focus on "getting a high score" — focus on:
1. Being the standout project for YOUR judges
2. Giving them material to advocate for you in caucus
3. Demonstrating understanding that makes you memorable
