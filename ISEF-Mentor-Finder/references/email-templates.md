# Outreach Email Templates

These are STARTING POINTS the skill fills in. The student MUST personalize before sending.

## Template 1 — Faculty research inquiry (most common case)

```
Subject: Inquiry about [SPECIFIC_TOPIC] research mentorship from a high school student

Dear Dr. [LASTNAME],

I'm [STUDENT_NAME], a [GRADE]-grade student at [SCHOOL] in [CITY]. I'm
writing because I'm developing a research project on [STUDENT_PROJECT_SUMMARY_ONE_SENTENCE]
and I read your recent paper [PAPER_TITLE] ([JOURNAL/CONFERENCE], [YEAR]) where
you [ONE_SPECIFIC_THING_FROM_THE_PAPER_THAT_CONNECTS]. That connection is what
made me reach out specifically to you rather than someone else in the field.

So far I've [STUDENT_PROGRESS_SO_FAR_TWO_SENTENCES — e.g., done a literature
review, built a pilot apparatus, run a preliminary experiment]. My current
question is [SPECIFIC_QUESTION] and I'm planning to submit this project to the
[FAIR — Regeneron ISEF / CASTIC / regional fair] in [MONTH, YEAR].

Would you be willing to meet for 20 minutes — in person or by video call — to
talk about whether my approach is on the right track? I know you're very busy
and no reply is a fine reply.

Thank you for your time.

Sincerely,
[STUDENT_FULL_NAME]
[GRADE], [SCHOOL]
[STUDENT_SCHOOL_EMAIL]
```

### What the student must change before sending
- `[SPECIFIC_TOPIC]` — not "biology" or "machine learning"; the actual phrase
- `[PAPER_TITLE]` — verify the paper exists; cite it correctly
- `[ONE_SPECIFIC_THING_FROM_THE_PAPER]` — read the paper or at least the abstract carefully
- `[STUDENT_PROGRESS_SO_FAR]` — be honest. If you've only read literature, say so. Faculty
  catch and dislike exaggeration.
- `[SPECIFIC_QUESTION]` — name what you actually want to know

### What to remove
- Anything in `[BRACKETS]` left unfilled
- Phrases that sound AI-generated ("I am writing to express my deep interest")
- Anything that's not true

## Template 2 — Industry researcher inquiry

```
Subject: HS student inquiry: [SPECIFIC_TOPIC] research interest

Dear Dr. [LASTNAME],

I'm a [GRADE]-grade student at [SCHOOL] working on a science fair project in
[TOPIC]. I came across your work at [COMPANY] on [SPECIFIC_PROJECT] via
[WHERE_FOUND — paper, talk, news article].

What interested me was [SPECIFIC_CONNECTION_ONE_SENTENCE]. I'd love to know
whether the approach we're taking — [SHORT_DESCRIPTION] — is consistent with
how this is done in industry.

If you have 15 minutes for a brief email exchange or call, I'd be grateful.
No reply is a fine reply; I understand you have many competing demands.

Thank you,
[STUDENT_FULL_NAME]
[GRADE], [SCHOOL]
[STUDENT_SCHOOL_EMAIL]
```

Note: industry researchers often face more legal constraints on advising students. Set
expectations accordingly — they may not be able to share proprietary information; they may
not be able to formally mentor outside their company. A 15-minute conversation is still
useful.

## Template 3 — Follow-up after 2 weeks of no reply (ONLY ONCE)

```
Subject: Re: Inquiry about [SPECIFIC_TOPIC] research mentorship

Dear Dr. [LASTNAME],

I wrote on [DATE_OF_FIRST_EMAIL] about my high school research project on
[TOPIC] and your paper [SHORT_PAPER_REFERENCE]. I know that time of year is
busy; I wanted to send one short follow-up in case the original email
slipped through.

If a brief conversation would be possible, I'd be grateful. If not, no
problem and I won't continue to follow up.

Thank you,
[STUDENT_FULL_NAME]
```

After this one follow-up, **stop**. Repeated emails to faculty about something they've
already received are negative signal.

## Template 4 — Response acknowledgment if they say "I can't mentor but here's an idea"

```
Dear Dr. [LASTNAME],

Thank you very much for the suggestion to [WHAT_THEY_SUGGESTED]. I'll
[FOLLOW_THROUGH_ACTION].

I really appreciate your time.

[STUDENT_NAME]
```

## Template 5 — Response if they say "Yes, let's chat"

```
Dear Dr. [LASTNAME],

Thank you for being willing to meet. I'm available:

  [DAY_1] at [TIME_1] (your time zone)
  [DAY_2] at [TIME_2]
  [DAY_3] at [TIME_3]

— please let me know which works best, or suggest a different time.

If video, I can use Zoom / Google Meet / Teams — please let me know which
is easiest for you.

In advance of the meeting, I'll send a one-page summary of my project
including the specific question I want your input on. That way we don't
spend the meeting time on background.

Thank you again.

[STUDENT_NAME]
```

The "one-page summary in advance" is critical — it shows respect for the mentor's time and
gets feedback on the actual question quickly.

## Anti-patterns the skill should reject

If the student-submitted personalization includes any of these patterns, flag them:

| Pattern | Fix |
|---|---|
| "I've read all your work" | Almost certainly false. Say what you actually read. |
| "I'm passionate about X" | Show, don't tell. What have you done about X? |
| "Will you be my mentor?" in first email | Too big an ask first email. Ask for 20 minutes first. |
| Attachments larger than 100KB | Don't attach without permission. Offer to send if interested. |
| Multiple researchers in CC | Send individual emails. CC'd emails feel mass-sent. |
| "I need to win ISEF" | Faculty don't care about your competition needs. Care about science. |
| "Sorry to bother you" | Don't apologize for reaching out. Confidence ≠ entitlement. |
| Email send time at 11pm | Send during work hours so it appears at the top of their morning inbox. |

## Length guidance

The whole email should fit in one short paragraph + a short ask + sign-off. Faculty get 100+
emails a day; long emails get archived.

Target: ≤ 200 words total. Better at 150.
