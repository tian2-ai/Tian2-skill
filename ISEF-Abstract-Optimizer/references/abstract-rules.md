## What "summarizes the current year's work" means

Book.pdf is explicit: the abstract is about THIS year's work. For continuation projects, prior
year's work goes in the attached prior abstract — not in this year's text. This is sometimes
violated by students who recap two years of work in one abstract.

## What CANNOT change after affiliated-fair competition

Book.pdf 286-291: "After competing in an Affiliated Fair, projects may not be changed or
amended." Once the SRC-approved abstract is the version competed-on, that's it. ISEF will reject
a modified version.

Implication for this skill: optimize BEFORE first competition. After that, optimization is
moot — you're locked in.

## Booth context (DS-Rules.pdf)

The abstract sits next to:
- ISEF Project Set-up Approval Form (signed on-site)
- Form 1C (if RRI work)
- Form 7 (if continuation)

All vertically displayed. Judges scan all four during their walk-by before the 15-minute
interview. The abstract is THE quick read — judges who haven't pre-read the project use it as
their orientation.

## What the rubric weighs

Per the official ISEF Science Project rubric (in `science-fair-judge/references/isef-rubric.md`):
- **Category V. Presentation = 35 points (Poster 10 + Interview 25)**

The abstract is a component of the 10-point poster score. Improving it improves at most 10
of 100 — but in close races (which most are), 1-2 of those points decide tier.

## Common SRC rejection reasons (anecdotal but consistent across affiliated-fair reports)

- Word count over 250 (auto-fail)
- Methods bloat (>1/3 of words on procedures)
- Missing significance / impact statement
- Vague claims without quantification
- Continuation undisclosed
- Mentor over-credited or under-credited
- AI use undisclosed
- Tense errors (future tense for completed work)
- Acronym not expanded on first use

These are the failure modes the optimizer's Step 3 checks against.
